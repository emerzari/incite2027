\section*{SIGNIFICANCE OF RESEARCH}
\vspace{-.2in}

Advanced nuclear reactors have emerged as a national priority due to their potential to provide reliable energy while enhancing U.S. energy security and technological leadership. Significant investments by the Department of Energy (DOE), industry, and national laboratories have accelerated the development of a diverse portfolio of advanced reactor concepts, including sodium-cooled fast reactors, molten salt reactors, gas-cooled reactors, microreactors, and fusion energy systems. Realizing the promise of these technologies, however, requires overcoming major challenges in design, safety analysis, licensing, and operational optimization.

A fundamental obstacle is the limited availability of integral-effect experimental data for many advanced reactor concepts. Because these systems often operate under conditions that differ substantially from those of the current reactor fleet, existing experimental databases are frequently insufficient for model development and validation. At the same time, the cost and complexity of large-scale testing make comprehensive experimental programs impractical during the early stages of technology development. Consequently, high-fidelity simulation has become an essential component of the advanced reactor development process, enabling direct investigation of complex thermal-fluid phenomena that cannot be adequately captured using traditional engineering-scale models alone.

This proposal supports a coordinated set of activities at Argonne National Laboratory and Idaho National Laboratory within the DOE Nuclear Energy Advanced Modeling and Simulation (NEAMS) program aimed at advancing the predictive capabilities of next-generation reactor analysis tools. The effort leverages leadership-class computing resources to perform large-eddy simulation (LES) and direct numerical simulation (DNS) of challenging reactor-relevant thermal-fluid phenomena, generating benchmark-quality datasets for advanced reactor applications. These simulations provide unprecedented insight into turbulent transport, thermal mixing, natural circulation, multiphase flows, and other phenomena that play a critical role in reactor safety and performance.

A central objective of the proposed work is the development of multiscale methodologies that bridge high-fidelity simulation and engineering-scale reactor analysis. While high-fidelity simulations provide the most accurate representation of the underlying physics, their computational cost precludes routine use in design and licensing studies. Therefore, an essential component of this effort is the systematic extraction of knowledge from DNS and LES calculations to improve lower-fidelity modeling approaches. The resulting datasets will be used to benchmark and validate reduced-order and system-scale models, quantify modeling uncertainties, and identify deficiencies in existing closures and constitutive relationships.

The project will also advance the development of computationally efficient surrogate and reduced-order modeling techniques. These efforts include both traditional approaches, such as Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) and related projection-based methods, as well as emerging machine-learning-based techniques capable of capturing complex nonlinear dynamics. By combining physics-based and data-driven methodologies, the project seeks to create scalable predictive tools that retain the essential physics captured by high-fidelity simulations while achieving the computational performance required for engineering applications.

The proposed research directly addresses one of the most important scientific and technological challenges facing advanced reactor deployment: the ability to translate detailed physical understanding into predictive, practical tools for reactor design, licensing, and operation. The resulting models, datasets, and methodologies will strengthen the NEAMS tool suite, support ongoing reactor development efforts across the DOE complex, and provide broadly applicable advances in multiscale modeling that will benefit a wide range of energy technologies. Access to INCITE resources is critical to achieving these objectives, as the required simulations represent some of the largest and most computationally demanding reactor thermal-fluid calculations currently feasible.

\subsection{Advancing Hight Temperature Gas Reactors Thermal-hydraulics}

The proposed research will employ high-fidelity computational fluid dynamics (CFD), including large-eddy simulation (LES), to improve the understanding of key thermal-hydraulic phenomena in high-temperature gas-cooled reactors (HTGRs). Experimental data from separate-effects test facilities will be used to validate the simulations and establish confidence in their predictive capability. Once validated, the high-fidelity simulations will provide benchmark-quality datasets that can be used to evaluate and improve lower-fidelity modeling approaches, including porous-media models, Reynolds-Averaged Navier--Stokes (RANS) methods, and system-level thermal-hydraulic tools. These activities directly support the development of multiscale modeling methodologies and the advancement of predictive reactor analysis capabilities within the DOE Nuclear Energy Advanced Modeling and Simulation (NEAMS) program.

The proposed work focuses on two thermal-fluid phenomena that are of particular importance for the performance and safety of HTGRs:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{Core Bypass Flow:} In both prismatic and pebble-bed HTGRs, the reflector region is composed of stacked graphite blocks. Manufacturing tolerances, assembly gaps, and irradiation-induced dimensional changes create flow pathways between adjacent blocks. These gaps allow a portion of the coolant to bypass the active core region and flow through the reflector instead, a phenomenon commonly referred to as \emph{core bypass flow}. Bypass flow can exceed 10\% of the total reactor mass flow rate and can significantly alter core flow distribution, core outlet temperatures, and reflector temperatures. Because these effects directly influence reactor performance and safety margins, accurate prediction of bypass flow is essential for reliable safety analysis and reactor design.

\item \textbf{Plenum-to-Plenum Natural Circulation:} Natural circulation plays a critical role in passive heat removal during HTGR pressurized loss-of-flow (PLOF) accidents. As coolant is heated within the core, buoyancy forces drive an upward flow toward the upper plenum. Heat transfer to cooler reflector regions subsequently generates downward flow paths through peripheral channels and reflector bypass regions, completing a circulation loop through the lower plenum. This process involves a complex interaction of buoyancy-driven flow, thermal stratification, plenum mixing, and bypass-channel transport. A detailed understanding of these phenomena is necessary for accurately predicting decay heat removal and assessing reactor safety during accident scenarios.

\end{itemize}

To investigate core bypass flow, the proposed work will utilize experimental data generated by Seoul National University (SNU) and the Korean Atomic Energy Research Institute (KAERI). These facilities (see Figure~\ref{f:experiments}) were specifically developed to characterize bypass flow under conditions representative of HTGR operation and include multiple test sections of varying geometric complexity. The experiments systematically investigate the effects of bypass gap size, flow rate, and block location, including both central and peripheral reflector configurations. The KAERI experiments operate at Reynolds numbers ranging from approximately 50,000 to 200,000, corresponding closely to reactor-relevant conditions.

High-fidelity LES calculations will be performed for these geometries and validated against the experimental measurements. Following validation, the resulting datasets will be used to benchmark and assess lower-fidelity modeling approaches, including RANS, porous-media, and system-level models. By examining a broad range of flow rates and gap configurations, the simulations will provide a comprehensive assessment of the capabilities and limitations of current engineering-scale approaches for predicting core bypass flow.

The study of plenum-to-plenum natural circulation will leverage two complementary experimental facilities. The first is a helium natural-circulation facility  (see Figure~\ref{f:experiments})  at the City College of New York (CCNY), consisting of upper and lower plena connected by eight vertical riser channels, with four heated and four cooled channels. Experiments have been conducted using both helium and nitrogen over a range of operating conditions and power distributions. The second facility, located at the Missouri University of Science and Technology, consists of a prismatic graphite core surrounded by four reflector bypass channels enclosed within upper and lower plena. This configuration is particularly relevant to emerging HTGR microreactor designs and enables investigation of natural-circulation behavior over a range of pressures and power levels.

Large-eddy simulations of both facilities will be performed to generate a comprehensive, validated database of buoyancy-driven flow and thermal-mixing phenomena relevant to both conventional and microreactor HTGR concepts. As with the bypass-flow investigations, these datasets will be used to evaluate and improve lower-fidelity modeling approaches, including RANS, porous-media, and system-level methods. The resulting benchmark data will support the development of improved multiscale models capable of accurately representing natural-circulation phenomena in reactor-scale safety analyses.

\section*{RESEARCH OBJECTIVES AND MILESTONES }  
\vspace{-.2in}

In the past 20 years the Nuclear Energy Advanced Modeling and Simulation (NEAMS) program \cite{sofu2017us} has invested in developing modern advanced Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) software to facilitate the deployment of advanced reactors. In fact, CFD is currently in widespread use both in reactor design and in safety analysis, as testified by the increasing number of articles in the field. Among recent examples, Roelofs \cite{roelofs2018thermal} illustrates the importance of CFD for liquid metal reactor design and analysis. In fact, detailed modeling and simulation is of particular importance for advanced reactors,  where it can be used in conjunction with separate effect experiments, in the absence of extensive integral test data (at least in the initial phases of development).

Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) \cite{conner2010cfd} and occasionally Unsteady Reynolds Averaged Navier Stokes (URANS) remain the workhorse for analysis conducted in industry, research centers, and academia.  However, despite significant advancements in turbulence modeling in recent years, RANS is limited in terms of accuracy. Turbulence modeling remains a source of uncertainty in complex engineering flows as RANS models are not general and are sensitive to even small geometric changes \cite{merzari2010numerical}. Moreover, RANS and URANS remain limited in predicting turbulent fluctuations and high-order statistics which may be of interest in important applications such as Flow-Induced Vibrations (FIV) and Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI) \cite{yuan2017flow}. In contrast to RANS, Wall-resolved Large Eddy Simulation (LES) and Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS) provide a much lesser degree of uncertainty, and they can provide valuable and unprecedented insight into flow physics. Historically, however, these methods have been limited to small geometries, such as sub-channels \cite{grotzbach1999direct} due to the high computational cost associated with both techniques. From the late 1990s to the end of the 2000s, LES/DNS remained a niche application for nuclear engineering flows. The review of Grotzbach \cite{grotzbach1999direct} provides a comprehensive status of the capabilities available in that time frame. Toward the end of the 2000s however larger-scale calculations started to emerge \cite{pointer2009simulations}. In fact, thanks to the advent of Petascale computing (i.e., computers capable of more than 1 Petaflop), the simulation of portions of nuclear components has been demonstrated with LES \cite{merzari2017large}.  These simulations can provide invaluable insight into the flow dynamics, which is difficult or often impossible to obtain with experiments alone. We note however that these datasets are often used in an ad-hoc manner and a lot of data is therefore wasted - a limitation that we plan to overcome through the multi-scale work planned as part of this project.

\subsection{Methods.}
\vspace{-.2in}

Let us consider first  the velocity, continuity, and energy  equations that describe the constant-property incompressible flow of a Newtonian using the Boussinesq approximation to model the buoyancy force:

\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial  u_i  }{\partial t} +  \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} \left( u_i u_j \right) =-\frac{1}{\rho} \frac{\partial p}{\partial x_i} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j} \left[ \nu \left( \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j} +\frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i} \right) \right] - \beta g_i (T-T_ref)
\label{UEqn}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_i} = 0
\label{rhoEqn}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\rho c_p \left( \frac{\partial T }{\partial t} + u_j \frac{\partial T}{\partial x_j} \right) = \frac{\partial }{\partial x_j} \left( \lambda \frac{\partial T}{\partial x_j} \right)
\label{EEqn}
\end{equation}
where $u$ is the velocity, $p$ is the pressure, $T$ is the temperature $\rho$ is the  density of the fluid, $\nu$ represents the kinematic viscosity, $\lambda$ is the thermal conductivity, $c_p$ is the heat capacity, $\beta$ is the expansion coefficient and $g_i$ is the gravity. \textit{The low Mach approximation can also be applied in Nek5000/NekRS}  \cite{tomboulides1997numerical} \textit{leading to a different formulation.} We restrict our discussion to turbulence-resolving simulations such as DNS and wall-resolved LES. We note that in advection-dominated problems such as the ones described by the equations presented at moderate to high Reynolds numbers, the high-wavenumber component of transported quantities does not decay exponentially, as observed in diffusion-dominated problems. Therefore, both meaningful signals and numerical errors can persist in time, and appropriate numerical schemes need to be selected in order to avoid excessive dispersion. Nek5000/NekRS, based on the spectral element method, is ideally suited for this type of analysis.
