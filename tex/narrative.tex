\section*{SIGNIFICANCE OF RESEARCH}
\vspace{-.2in}

Advanced nuclear reactors have emerged as a national priority due to their potential to provide reliable energy while enhancing U.S. energy security and technological leadership. Significant investments by the Department of Energy (DOE), industry, and national laboratories have accelerated the development of a diverse portfolio of advanced reactor concepts, including sodium-cooled fast reactors, molten salt reactors, gas-cooled reactors, microreactors, and fusion energy systems. Realizing the promise of these technologies, however, requires overcoming major challenges in design, safety analysis, licensing, and operational optimization.

A fundamental obstacle is the limited availability of integral-effect experimental data for many advanced reactor concepts. Because these systems often operate under conditions that differ substantially from those of the current reactor fleet, existing experimental databases are frequently insufficient for model development and validation. At the same time, the cost and complexity of large-scale testing make comprehensive experimental programs impractical during the early stages of technology development. Consequently, high-fidelity simulation has become an essential component of the advanced reactor development process, enabling direct investigation of complex thermal-fluid phenomena that cannot be adequately captured using traditional engineering-scale models alone.

This proposal supports a coordinated set of activities at Argonne National Laboratory and Idaho National Laboratory within the DOE Nuclear Energy Advanced Modeling and Simulation (NEAMS) program aimed at advancing the predictive capabilities of next-generation reactor analysis tools. The effort leverages leadership-class computing resources to perform large-eddy simulation (LES) and direct numerical simulation (DNS) of challenging reactor-relevant thermal-fluid phenomena, generating benchmark-quality datasets for advanced reactor applications. These simulations provide unprecedented insight into turbulent transport, thermal mixing, natural circulation, multiphase flows, and other phenomena that play a critical role in reactor safety and performance.

A central objective of the proposed work is the development of multiscale methodologies that bridge high-fidelity simulation and engineering-scale reactor analysis. While high-fidelity simulations provide the most accurate representation of the underlying physics, their computational cost precludes routine use in design and licensing studies. Therefore, an essential component of this effort is the systematic extraction of knowledge from DNS and LES calculations to improve lower-fidelity modeling approaches. The resulting datasets will be used to benchmark and validate reduced-order and system-scale models, quantify modeling uncertainties, and identify deficiencies in existing closures and constitutive relationships.

The project will also advance the development of computationally efficient surrogate and reduced-order modeling techniques. These efforts include both traditional approaches, such as Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) and related projection-based methods, as well as emerging machine-learning-based techniques capable of capturing complex nonlinear dynamics. By combining physics-based and data-driven methodologies, the project seeks to create scalable predictive tools that retain the essential physics captured by high-fidelity simulations while achieving the computational performance required for engineering applications.

The proposed research directly addresses one of the most important scientific and technological challenges facing advanced reactor deployment: the ability to translate detailed physical understanding into predictive, practical tools for reactor design, licensing, and operation. The resulting models, datasets, and methodologies will strengthen the NEAMS tool suite, support ongoing reactor development efforts across the DOE complex, and provide broadly applicable advances in multiscale modeling that will benefit a wide range of energy technologies. Access to INCITE resources is critical to achieving these objectives, as the required simulations represent some of the largest and most computationally demanding reactor thermal-fluid calculations currently feasible.
